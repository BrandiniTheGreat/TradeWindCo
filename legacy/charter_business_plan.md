# Caribbean Charter Catamaran Acquisition Company — Business Plan Summary

## Document Purpose
This document is a comprehensive summary of market research, financial modeling, and strategic planning conducted for a BVI-based catamaran charter and resale business. It is intended as source material for generating a formal SBA loan application, business plan, and investor pitch deck.

---

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

### The Opportunity
A market dislocation created by 2025 US import tariffs (20-25% on foreign-built vessels) has stranded hundreds of ex-charter catamarans in the British Virgin Islands. These boats — previously valued at $459,000-$700,000+ new — are now available for $275,000-$400,000 through fleet brokerage liquidation. American buyers, who represent the largest demand pool, are largely unwilling to absorb the import duty required to bring these boats stateside.

### The Business Model
The company acquires undervalued ex-fleet catamarans in the BVI, immediately places them into performance charter fleet programs to generate recurring revenue, and simultaneously offers them for sale to US retail buyers at a markup — handling the import logistics, customs, and delivery as a value-add service.

### The Dual Revenue Engine
- **Charter Revenue (recurring):** Each boat earns $50,000-80,000/year in net operating income through a managed bareboat charter fleet in the BVI. This covers all debt service and operating expenses with margin.
- **Resale Revenue (episodic):** Each boat can be sold to a US buyer at $150,000-200,000+ gross margin after import costs. The boat earns charter revenue while waiting for a buyer, eliminating carrying cost risk.

### Key Advantage
The boat is never a liability. It earns revenue from day one in charter. If it sells quickly, the company profits on the spread. If it takes months, the charter income covers all costs. The company makes money whether it sells or not.

## 2. MARKET ANALYSIS

### 2.1 BVI Charter Market
- The BVI accounts for 40% of the entire Caribbean's professional charter fleet — 685 vessels (Source: 12knots.com, 2026)
- Over 56% of the BVI charter fleet is under 3 years old
- The BVI welcomed 1,092,139 visitors in 2024, up 9.8% from 2023 (BVI Government, Jan 2025)
- Caribbean catamaran bookings grew 15% year-over-year in 2024 (Mordor Intelligence, 2025)
- Crewed catamarans are the fastest-growing segment of the charter market
- The global yacht charter market hit $8.98 billion in 2025, growing at 8.19% annually (Fortune Business Insights, 2026)
- BVI has zero charter taxes, short inter-island distances, and the largest professional charter fleet in the Caribbean

### 2.2 Charter Rates — 45-50ft Catamarans, Caribbean
- **Bareboat high season (Dec-Apr):** $7,000-$15,000/week for 4-cabin catamaran
- **Bareboat low season (May-Nov):** $3,500-$5,700/week
- **Bareboat blended average:** $4,800-$5,500/week
- **Crewed (captain only) 45-50ft:** $17,000-$24,000/week
- **Crewed (captain + chef) 50-55ft all-inclusive:** $25,000-$35,000/week
- Average weekly catamaran charter cost in Caribbean: $5,862 (12knots.com, sample of 40-50ft boats)

### 2.3 Occupancy Rates
- Catamarans globally: 85% average occupancy vs 75% for monohulls (Booking Manager, 2025)
- Industry benchmark for successful charter yacht: 10-12 weeks/year (luxury segment), 22-30 weeks/year (fleet-managed bareboat)
- Elite charter businesses target 70-80% during peak, 50-60% off-peak
- Realistic blended annual for well-positioned Caribbean cat: 60-70%
- Year 1 estimate: 45-55% (ramp-up, building reviews)
- Year 2-3 steady state: 60-70%
- Peak mature boat: 70-80%
- Bad year (hurricane, recession): 35-45%

### 2.4 Tariff Situation Creating the Opportunity
- As of April 2025, US imposed reciprocal tariffs: base 10% on most imports, with elevated rates by country
- South African-built boats (Leopard/Robertson & Caine): ~20-25% tariff rate
- French-built boats (Lagoon, Bali, Fountaine Pajot): ~20% tariff rate
- Previous duty rate: 1.5%
- This 10-20x increase in import costs has frozen the US buyer market for foreign-built boats sitting offshore
- Result: oversupply of used catamarans in BVI, depressed brokerage prices, motivated sellers
- Key insight: boats operating in charter programs in foreign waters (BVI) are NOT subject to US import tariffs

### 2.5 Customs Valuation — Critical for Resale Model
- US CBP assesses duty on "Transaction Value" — the price actually paid by the buyer, NOT fair market value
- A boat purchased for $309,000 in the BVI and imported to the US pays duty on $309,000, not the $550-600K US retail value
- This is codified in CBP valuation regulations and is the standard primary method
- The spread between acquisition cost and retail value, minus import duty, is the company's margin

## 7. BUSINESS STRUCTURE

### 7.1 Entity
- Florida LLC (e.g., "[Name] Charter Holdings LLC")
- Boat registered under LLC as business asset
- US-flagged, operating in BVI (no import duty triggered while boat stays offshore)
- Alternative: foreign flag (Marshall Islands/BVI) if flexibility to bring boat to US waters temporarily is desired — operates under US Customs cruising license

### 7.2 Partnership Structure
- Two co-owners, 50/50 equity split
- Both personally guarantee SBA loan (required for 20%+ owners)
- Operating agreement governs: capital contributions, decision rights, distribution waterfall, exit provisions, dispute resolution
- Partner with captain's license receives labor compensation as a business expense (separate from equity split)

### 7.3 Distribution Waterfall (LLC Operating Agreement)
1. **First priority:** Loan payments — marine mortgage and SBA, automatic monthly
2. **Second priority:** Operating reserves — no distributions until LLC holds 6+ months of operating costs (~$24,000-$25,000)
3. **Third priority:** Friends/family investor repayment — all excess cash flow until promissory notes are fully repaid with interest
4. **Fourth priority:** Partner distributions — only after investors repaid and reserves funded, split per equity agreement

### 7.4 Tax Structure
- Yacht operated as active business through LLC qualifies for business tax deductions
- Deductible: depreciation (MACRS or Section 179/bonus depreciation), loan interest, insurance, maintenance, operating expenses, travel for inspection/due diligence
- Charter income reported as business income on US tax returns
- Maritime CPA required for proper structuring — estimated $2,000-$3,000/year in specialized tax prep
- Personal use weeks must be carefully documented vs. business use to maintain IRS compliance

## 8. RISK ANALYSIS

### 8.1 Risks and Mitigations

| Risk | Severity | Probability | Mitigation |
|------|----------|-------------|------------|
| Hurricane damage/total loss | High | Medium | Agreed-value hull insurance; insurance payout covers debt stack; LLC reserves for deductible (2-5% of hull value) |
| Low occupancy (<55%) | High | Low-Medium | Conservative underwriting; 55% breakeven is well below 60-70% industry average; multiple fleet managers available |
| Engine failure | Medium | Medium | Budget $30-40K repower; engines at 3,000 hrs have 3-5 years of life remaining; maintenance reserves |
| Partnership dispute | High | Low | Comprehensive operating agreement drafted by maritime attorney before any money moves |
| Tariff reduction (kills flip margin) | Medium | Medium | Charter revenue continues regardless; flip business is upside, not required for survival |
| Fleet manager underperformance | Medium | Low | Pre-negotiated exit clauses; multiple alternative managers available in BVI |
| Interest rate increase on variable debt | Low | Low | Fixed-rate marine mortgage; SBA typically fixed |

### 8.2 Insurance Requirements
- Agreed-value hull insurance (NOT actual cash value) — insured at $300,000+
- Named storm/hurricane coverage (critical for Caribbean)
- Separate windstorm deductible: typically 2-5% of hull value ($6,000-$15,000)
- Liability coverage
- All lienholders (marine mortgage, SBA) listed on policy
- Estimated annual premium: $7,000-$9,000

### 8.3 Worst Case Scenario
- Hurricane totals the boat
- Insurance pays agreed hull value
- Payout covers marine mortgage (primary lienholder), then SBA, then investors
- LLC closes, partners walk away having lost time but limited personal capital
- Personal guarantee exposure limited to any gap between insurance payout and outstanding loan balances — typically minimal with proper agreed-value coverage

## 10. COMPETITIVE ADVANTAGES

1. **Tariff arbitrage:** Buying at depressed BVI prices while operating in a tariff-exempt use case
2. **Dual revenue model:** Charter income eliminates carrying cost risk that kills traditional flip businesses
3. **Captain-partner:** Licensed captain as co-owner reduces crew costs, enables crewed charter premium, provides operational expertise
4. **Known inventory:** Ex-Moorings/Sunsail boats have complete maintenance records, known histories, and proven charter demand
5. **Location:** BVI is the #1 charter market globally with the deepest demand pool
6. **Import service moat:** Willingness to handle customs, delivery, and import logistics is a real value-add that most individual buyers won't do themselves

## 13. REFERENCE: MOORINGS/DREAM YACHT GUARANTEED PROGRAMS (Alternative Model)

If the performance/resale model is not pursued, guaranteed income programs offer a lower-risk alternative:

### The Moorings Guaranteed Income
- 8% of purchase price paid annually, guaranteed regardless of charter activity
- All maintenance, insurance, dockage, repairs covered by Moorings
- Up to 12 weeks personal sailing/year
- 5-6 year contract
- Requires purchasing NEW Leopard catamaran through Moorings
- Backed by KKR/Travelopia ($578B AUM)
- Continued paying through Hurricane Irma and Covid-19

### Dream Yacht Charter Guarantee
- 7-8% guaranteed annual income
- Can trade owner weeks for higher guaranteed rate
- 42-66 month contract
- Wider brand selection (Lagoon, Bali, Fountaine Pajot, Beneteau)
- Backed by PPF Group + Beneteau Group

### Guaranteed Program Math (Leopard 45, $629K)
- 8% guaranteed = $50,320/year = $4,193/month
- Mortgage (20% down, 15yr @ 8%): $4,809/month
- Monthly gap: -$616/month
- At 30.2% down: gap = $0 (dead even)
- Above 30.2% down: cash flow positive

**Comparison:** The guaranteed programs offer safety and predictability but require buying new (higher cost) and cap upside. The performance/resale model using used boats offers significantly higher returns but carries variable income risk.
